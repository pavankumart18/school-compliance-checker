Act I · The Problem

A school that doesn't know
what it doesn't know

Every morning, North Broward Preparatory School in Coral Springs opens its doors to 847 students. Windermere Preparatory School in Orlando does the same for 680 more. Both are part of Nord Anglia Education — one of the world's largest private school groups. And both are simultaneously subject to a regulatory framework so fragmented, so spread across state codes, federal rules, and agency guidelines, that no single person at either school could recite it in full.

This is not a failure of diligence. It is a structural problem. The regulations governing a private international school in Florida don't live in one place. They are scattered across Florida Statutes Chapter 1002, the Florida Administrative Code, Title 8 of the Code of Federal Regulations, the Fair Labor Standards Act, IRS tax code, OSHA standards, EPA asbestos rules, FTC guidelines, and a half-dozen agency handbooks — each updated on its own cycle, each enforced by a different office.

“
"The school had the right policies. It simply lacked the system to track them."
Core finding — Windermere compliance audit

The question we set out to answer was simple: could an AI read this entire regulatory landscape — from raw statutes to agency handbooks — and produce a structured, machine-readable compliance schema that a software engine could actually execute?

The answer required two things. First, a very precisely engineered research prompt. Second, the willingness to treat the AI's output not as a document to read, but as a database to run.

Act II · The Prompt

Writing a question
the law could answer

The first and most important step was not running an AI search. It was crafting a research brief precise enough that the AI would return structured, auditable data — not a general overview. This took careful thought about what we actually needed.

Most AI research prompts ask for a summary. We needed something different: for each regulation, we needed the exact statute number, the specific form required, the inspection interval, which agency enforces it, and — critically — what a software engine could verify. Not "schools must maintain safe facilities." Rather: "Form 2020-IF, every 30 school days, Rule 6A-3.0171, flag if days since last inspection > 30."

The prompt was structured into five specific research mandates, each targeting a different regulatory domain, and it demanded output in a precise format.

Two things make this prompt different from a typical search query. First, it is domain-specific — it names exact statute numbers, specific form identifiers, and known regulatory sub-domains. This forces the AI to go beyond surface summaries and into the actual legal text. Second, it demands a specific output schema — not prose, but structured columns that can be directly ingested into a database.

The key design insight Asking an AI to "research Florida school regulations" produces a report. Asking it to produce "Category | Requirement | Citation | Proof Document | Deadline | Recipient Agency" for each rule produces a database. The output format is as important as the question itself.

The prompt was run through Gemini Deep Research, which spent approximately 20 minutes traversing Florida DOE resources, CFR databases, FLDOE handbooks, DOH form repositories, OSHA standards, and more. What came back was not a document. It was a compliance architecture.

The "Gotcha" findings

Beyond the structured schema, the AI surfaced several non-obvious regulatory nuances that a manual research process would likely miss entirely:

What schools typically assume
Private schools follow the same teacher certification rules as public schools → Wrong
FERPA applies to all private schools automatically → Wrong
Radon testing is an ongoing annual requirement → Partially wrong
Bus inspections run on a calendar-month cycle → Wrong
Scholarship funds = state funds = no federal privacy triggers → Mostly right, with traps
A for-profit school files taxes like a nonprofit → Wrong
Large cash tuition payments are a business matter → Criminal exposure
What the AI extracted from the law
OR gate: Bachelor's degree || 3yr experience || special_skills → s.1002.421(1)(h)
FERPA triggers only on direct federal funding — FTC/FES scholarships are exempt
Mandatory twice (5yr apart), then stops unless remodel == TRUE → s.404.056
Every 30 school days — not calendar days → Rule 6A-3.0171, Form 2020-IF
FERPA exempt, BUT SOPIPA (s.1006.1494) applies regardless — vendor DPAs required
For-profit entity: no Form 990. Must file BOI with FinCEN + Form 8300 for cash > $10k
Cash > $10,000 → Form 8300 within 15 days → failure = federal felony 26 U.S.C. 6050I
The "For-Profit Trap" Nord Anglia schools are owned by EQT/Neuberger Berman — a private equity firm. This makes them for-profit corporate subsidiaries, not nonprofits. The AI flagged this immediately: all 501(c)(3) logic (Form 990s, etc.) must be replaced with corporate compliance logic including BOI filings, Form 8300 cash reporting, and W-8BEN for foreign contractors. Most generic compliance frameworks completely miss this.

Act V · The Engine

Schema becomes
working software

With the schema extracted, the final step was building the compliance engine. The AI-generated schema becomes the checklist.json — a structured file that the engine reads to know what to check. The school's PDF documents become the evidence base. A series of LLM calls evaluates each regulation against the documents, returning a JSON object for every check.

// Step 1: Load the AI-extracted schema const checklist = await fetch("./data/checklist.json").then(r => r.json()) // 72 regulations, each with: id, trigger, validation_logic, citation, artifact // Step 2: For each school, load their submitted documents const school_docs = [ "Annual_Survey_2025.pdf", // → checks ADM-01, ADM-02, ADM-03 "VECHS_Clearance_Log.pdf", // → checks PER-01 "SEVIS_I20_Log.pdf", // → checks IMM-01, IMM-02 "Financial_Statements.pdf", // → checks FIN-01, IRS-01, IRS-02 "Bus_Inspection_Form_2020IF.pdf" // → checks TRN-01, TRN-02 ] // Step 3: Chunk checklist → multiple LLM calls → reduce hallucination for (const chunk of chunkify(checklist, 10)) { const result = await callLLM({ docs: school_docs, rules: chunk, instruction: "Evaluate each rule. Return JSON array: {id, status, evidence, quote, action}" }) streamToUI(result) // → each row appears in real time } // Step 4: Each result object → one row in the compliance dashboard // status: "compliant" | "partial" | "non-compliant" // evidence: why compliant, or what's missing // quote: verbatim citation from the document (if compliant) // action: what to do if non-compliant

Why it works: evidence, not assertion

The critical design principle is that every compliance determination must be cited, not just stated. When the engine marks a school compliant on VECHS screening, it must quote the exact text from the document that proves it. When it flags a violation, it names the statute, explains what's wrong, and tells the administrator exactly what to do.

This is what makes the AI extraction step non-negotiable. Without a precisely structured schema derived from actual legal text, the engine has nothing to check against. The prompt wasn't a search. It was a specification.

“
"The output format is as important as the question. Ask for a report and you get prose. Ask for a schema and you get a database."
Design principle — ComplianceIQ

The result, across the two schools, was striking. North Broward returned a compliance score of 87% — strong on personnel and immigration, with three technical failures in environmental compliance. Windermere scored 61% — seven non-compliances, all administrative failures: a late survey, an expired VECHS clearance still on campus, a missed scholarship deadline. Not failures of intent. Failures of system.

Every single one of those failures is now impossible to miss. That is what the machine learned to see.

STATE
The "OR Gate" Teacher Rule
Florida doesn't require state certification. A teacher qualifies with a degree OR 3 years experience OR documented special skills. The AI correctly identified this as a three-way OR condition — not a simple credential check.
s. 1002.421(1)(h), F.S. · Trigger: Role == "Teacher"
FEDERAL
The Radon "Stop Rule"
Most compliance tools treat radon as annual. The AI found it's mandatory only twice — initial test, then 5 years later. After that, testing stops unless the building is remodeled. Most schools either over-test or forget the second test entirely.
s. 404.056, F.S. · IF Tests_Done ≥ 2: Required = FALSE
FEDERAL
The For-Profit Switch
Because Nord Anglia is owned by private equity, it's a corporate subsidiary — not a nonprofit. This flips entire categories: no Form 990, but mandatory BOI filing with FinCEN, Form 8300 for cash tuition payments, and W-8BEN for foreign contractors.
31 CFR 1010.380 · 26 U.S.C. 6050I · IRS Treas. Reg. 1.1441-1
STATE
The FERPA Trap
Florida scholarship funds (FTC, FES) are state tax-credit funds — not federal funds. So FERPA does not apply. BUT Florida's own SOPIPA statute applies regardless, requiring vendor contracts to prohibit data selling. A subtlety most generic tools miss.
s. 1006.1494, F.S. (SOPIPA) · Applies even without FERPA trigger
STATE
The 30-Day School Clock
Bus inspections run every 30 school days — not calendar days. This is the most frequently missed transportation requirement. A school with 180 school days needs 6 inspections per year, not 12 calendar-month checks. The AI flagged this distinction explicitly.
Rule 6A-3.0171, F.A.C. · Form 2020-IF · IF school_days > 30: OUT OF SERVICE
FEDERAL
The Cash Felony
International schools routinely receive large cash tuition payments from overseas families. Any single cash transaction over $10,000 triggers mandatory Form 8300 filing within 15 days. Missing it isn't a fine. It's a federal felony under 26 U.S.C. 6050I.
26 U.S.C. 6050I · IF cash > $10,000: File within 15 days or face criminal charges
Conclusion

The prompt was
the first step

The story of this compliance engine begins not with code, but with a question — precisely engineered to extract structured data from legal text. The AI did not write the regulations. It read them, translated them, and handed back a schema that could be executed by a machine.

That schema — 72 regulations, 11 agencies, 18 categories — now powers a live compliance dashboard for two Florida schools. Every red flag it surfaces is the direct product of a legal text the AI read, a validation rule it wrote, and a school document it checked. The machine learned to read the law so that the humans running the school don't have to.
